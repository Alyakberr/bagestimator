Figuring Out How Much Concrete You Actually Need

Every concrete job comes down to the same basic question: how much material do I need to buy so I don't end up short on a Saturday afternoon, or stuck with a dozen leftover bags taking up space in the garage? The honest answer is that it depends on more than just length times width times depth. Bag yield varies by mix and manufacturer, waste from spillage and uneven forms adds up fast, and soil conditions can throw off hole-based projects more than people expect. This calculator handles the math, but it helps to understand what's actually going on behind the numbers.

Slabs, Patios, and Footings

For a flat pour like a patio, walkway, or shed pad, the calculation starts simple: length times width times thickness gives you the raw volume. Where people usually get tripped up is thickness consistency. Even a well-formed slab rarely comes out perfectly uniform, since sub-grade prep, gravel base depth, and minor form flex all shift the real pour volume slightly higher than the on-paper number. That's why a 10 percent waste margin is baked into the results here rather than left for you to eyeball afterward.

Bag size matters more than people think, too. An 80lb bag yields about 0.6 cubic feet once mixed, a 60lb bag yields around 0.45, and a 40lb bag yields roughly 0.3. If you're doing a large pour, 80lb bags are usually more cost-efficient per cubic foot, but they're also considerably heavier to move and mix by hand — worth factoring in if you're working solo.

Post Holes and Footings

Post hole math looks straightforward until you remember that the post itself is sitting inside the hole, displacing part of the volume you'd otherwise fill with concrete. A 4x4 piece of lumber isn't actually 4 inches by 4 inches — it's closer to 3.5 by 3.5 once it's milled — and a 6x6 measures about 5.5 by 5.5. Skipping that subtraction is one of the most common reasons people overbuy bags for fence and deck projects. Frost depth is the other variable worth checking locally before you dig; colder regions often require holes well below what a mild-climate project would need, which changes your volume more than the diameter does.

Trench and Subbase Material

Trench fill is priced and sold by weight rather than bag count, so the math shifts from volume to tonnage. Crushed stone runs roughly 1.4 tons per cubic yard, sand closer to 1.3, and plain fill dirt around 1.2 — the exact number depends on how compacted the material is when it's delivered versus how compacted it'll be once you've tamped it down. A trench that looks like it needs half a ton of gravel can easily need a full ton once you account for settling, which is why the compaction margin is included by default rather than something you have to remember to add.

Common Questions People Ask Before Pouring

Do I need to wet the ground before pouring concrete?

Lightly dampening dry, sandy, or highly absorbent soil before a pour is common practice, since bone-dry ground can pull moisture out of the concrete mix too quickly and weaken the cure. It shouldn't be muddy or standing in water — just enough that the surface isn't dusty.

Can I mix regular playground sand instead of buying premix bags?

No — premix bags already contain the correct ratio of cement, sand, and aggregate for structural strength. Substituting loose sand on its own skips the cement binder entirely and won't cure into anything load-bearing.

How thick should a concrete slab be for a shed or storage building?

Most small storage sheds are fine on a 4 inch slab, but anything supporting a vehicle, a larger workshop, or heavier equipment usually calls for 5 to 6 inches, sometimes with rebar or wire mesh reinforcement. Local building codes and frost line depth can also push that number up.

How long should concrete cure before I put weight on it?

Concrete is typically firm enough to walk on after about 24 to 48 hours, but it isn't considered fully cured for structural loads like vehicles or heavy furniture until around 28 days, since strength continues building well past the point where it looks and feels solid.

Why did my actual bag count come in higher than the calculator estimate?

The most common causes are uneven excavation depth, gravel base that ate into your usable hole or slab depth, or a wider trench/hole than originally measured. Measuring twice at multiple points rather than once at the center will usually close that gap.
